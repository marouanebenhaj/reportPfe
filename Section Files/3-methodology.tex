\section{METHODOLOGY}
\label{sec:methodology}

\noindent
This section explains how the research is organized, how the dataset is built, and what tools are used. It covers not just the technical pipeline but the reasoning behind the design choices: why each step is done the way it is, and what motivated the move from one experimental iteration to the next.

\subsection{Design Science Research Methodology}

\noindent
This project follows the DSR methodology \cite{wieringa2014design}. The core idea behind DSR is that in applied computing, useful knowledge is generated by building things and evaluating them against real problems, not by observing systems from the outside. The primary output of a DSR study is a working artifact, assessed for how well it solves the problem it was designed for. This sets DSR apart from explanatory research, where the goal is to describe or understand a phenomenon rather than to intervene in it.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent
The artifact in this study is a supervised regression system: a neural network that maps a mel spectrogram computed from an audio clip, together with a normalized MIDI pitch, to a 16-dimensional parameter vector for the Vital synthesizer. The problem it addresses has no clean analytical solution, and Vital itself is non-differentiable, which rules out gradient-based optimization through the rendering process. DSR fits this context because the research involves genuine design work at every stage: choosing how to represent audio, selecting and adapting model architectures, constructing a dataset generation strategy, and revising those choices based on what evaluation results reveal.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent
The three experimental iterations, V1, V2, and V3, each correspond to a complete DSR cycle. V1 established a working baseline and identified ConvNeXt as the strongest backbone. V2 introduced multi-scale mel input, FiLM pitch conditioning, and a grouped prediction head, then ran a controlled ablation to measure each component's contribution individually. V3 responded to a concrete finding from V2: pitch conditioning had almost no measurable effect when every preset was rendered at only one MIDI pitch. Expanding the dataset to three pitches per preset was a direct response to that failure. That feedback loop, where an evaluation result reshapes the next design rather than confirming a prior assumption, is the DSR cycle in practice.

\subsubsection{Problem Investigation}

The literature review in Section 2 surveyed inverse synthesis, audio deep learning, and synthesizer parameter estimation, and identified four gaps that directly motivate this work.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent (i) \textbf{Wavetable synthesizers remain under-studied relative to FM and subtractive ones.} Prior deep learning work has been applied to wavetable and hybrid synthesizers, including Native Instruments Massive \cite{bruford2024ast} and SurgeXT \cite{hayes2025surgext}. However, no prior work has studied Vital's specific 16-parameter scope with the combination of multi-scale mel input, explicit pitch conditioning, and a systematic architecture comparison. The parameter set used here, drawn from the classic analog signal chain and adapted to Vital's wavetable oscillators, is studied for the first time in this configuration.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent (ii) \textbf{Existing approaches use a single mel-spectrogram scale.} The primary baseline \cite{bruford2024ast} and the broader literature both compute a single mel spectrogram per audio clip. Since the 16 parameters span time scales from millisecond-range envelope attacks to slow timbral evolution, no single FFT window can represent all of them well. This resolution trade-off has not been addressed in prior work.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent (iii) \textbf{No prior work conditions on pitch.} Models in the literature receive only the audio spectrogram as input. The same synthesizer preset rendered at different MIDI pitches produces spectrograms that differ substantially in their harmonic structure, yet these differences carry no parameter information. Without an explicit pitch input, the model must either memorize the pitch-timbre confound or ignore pitch-sensitive parameters. None of the prior approaches addresses this.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent (iv) \textbf{ConvNeXt has not been evaluated for this task.} Bruford et al.\ compared AST against MLP and CNN baselines \cite{bruford2024ast}, but did not include ConvNeXt, a purely convolutional architecture that matches or exceeds vision transformers on standard image benchmarks \cite{liu2022convnext}. Whether ConvNeXt or AST is better suited to synthesizer spectrogram regression is an open question that this work addresses directly.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent Taken together, these gaps define a problem that is not just technically hard but also practically relevant: sound designers working with Vital have no automated tool to recover the parameters behind a sound they want to replicate. The three research questions from Section 1 map directly onto gaps (ii), (iii), and (iv) above.

\subsubsection{Solution Design}

\noindent \textbf{Dataset design.} Sampling parameters uniformly at random from Vital's full range can lead to silent, clipping, or tonally incoherent sounds, which carry no useful signal for a regression model. To address this, a timbre-constrained generation strategy was adopted: five categories (bass, lead, pad, percussion, and random) each have a dedicated range file that restricts per-parameter sampling to musically coherent sub-ranges, paired with a corresponding MIDI pitch range for that timbre. Together, the parameter constraints and pitch ranges ensure that random draws within those bounds consistently produce recognizable, musically plausible sounds. The parameter space was scoped to 16 controls drawn from the classic analog signal chain of the Minimoog and Prophet-5, giving the regression task a clear structure without being so narrow that the results are trivial.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent \textbf{Input representation design.} A mel spectrogram is the natural starting point, but no single FFT window size works well for all 16 parameters. Short windows resolve attack transients well but blur harmonic content; long windows do the opposite. Rather than picking one and discarding the rest, three spectrograms are computed at fine, mid, and coarse resolutions, each using a different FFT window size and hop length, and stacked as a three-channel input tensor, letting each scale contribute the information it captures best.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent \textbf{Architecture design.} In V1, three backbone families were implemented: a custom residual CNN, a pre-trained ConvNeXt, and a pre-trained AST. Pitch information was incorporated via a sinusoidal embedding concatenated to the backbone features. In V2, the CNN was dropped and the study focused on ConvNeXt and AST. Both backbones were augmented with a FiLM pitch conditioning branch, which modulates the spectrogram features multiplicatively using the normalized MIDI pitch rather than appending it as a side channel, and a grouped prediction head that assigns a wider sub-MLP to oscillator parameters than to envelope, filter, or miscellaneous parameters.

\subsubsection{Design Validation}

Validation in this project operates at two levels: comparing iterations against each other, and isolating individual components within an iteration.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent At the iteration level, each experimental increment is validated by comparing training and test MAE before and after the change. V1 establishes the reference: CNN, ConvNeXt, and AST are trained on single-scale mel spectrograms with sinusoidal pitch concatenation, and both training and test MAE are recorded for each. V2 introduces three architectural changes simultaneously — multi-scale mel input, FiLM pitch conditioning, and the grouped prediction head — and the same metrics are recorded for ConvNeXt and AST. The V1-to-V2 MAE gap measures the combined effect of those architectural innovations. V3 then changes the data rather than the architecture, introducing multi-pitch rendering and SpecAugment, and the V2-to-V3 gap measures the effect of the expanded and augmented dataset.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent At the component level, a controlled ablation study within V2 isolates the contribution of each architectural innovation. The logic is straightforward: start with the full V2 model with all components active, then for each variant, disable or simplify exactly one component and measure how much the test MAE degrades. The gap between the full model and a variant directly quantifies what that component contributes. All variants use ConvNeXt-tiny as the backbone and are trained under identical conditions. \par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent A third validation axis is provided by the Multi-Scale Spectral (MSS) loss. After training is complete, V2 and V3 models are evaluated on the same shared 100-sample test set by re-rendering their predicted parameters through Vital and measuring the spectral distance between the original and re-rendered audio. This provides a perceptual quality measure that is independent of the parameter-space MAE and captures whether the predicted parameters actually reproduce the target sound.

\subsubsection{Solution Implementation}

\noindent \textbf{V1.} A dataset of single-scale mel spectrograms was generated, one per audio clip at the mid scale. Three backbone families were trained and compared under identical conditions: a custom residual CNN, ConvNeXt-tiny, and AST-small. Each backbone was tested both with and without a sinusoidal pitch embedding appended to the backbone features via concatenation. Training and test MAE were recorded for each configuration.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent \textbf{V2.} Three architectural changes were introduced: three-channel multi-scale mel input stacking fine, mid, and coarse spectrograms; FiLM pitch conditioning replacing the concatenation-based approach; and a grouped prediction head with 2$\times$ loss weighting on oscillator parameters. The CNN was dropped and only ConvNeXt-tiny and AST-small were carried forward. The five ablation variants described in Section~\ref{sec:methodology} were trained alongside the main models.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent \textbf{V3.} The model architecture was kept identical to V2. The dataset was expanded by rendering each preset at three fixed MIDI pitches per timbre rather than one, growing the total from approximately 42{,}000 to 127{,}000 samples. SpecAugment was added during training, randomly masking strips along the time and frequency axes of the mel spectrogram at each iteration.

\subsubsection{Solution Evaluation}

The primary evaluation metric is MAE in the normalised parameter space. After min-max normalisation, all 16 parameters lie in $[0, 1]$, so the overall MAE for a model on a test set of $N$ samples is:

\begin{equation}
\text{MAE} = \frac{1}{16N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{k=1}^{16} \left|\hat{x}_{i,k} - x_{i,k}\right|
\label{eq:mae}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $x_{i,k}$ is the ground-truth normalised value of parameter $k$ for sample $i$ and $\hat{x}_{i,k}$ is the model's prediction.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent Results are reported as mean $\pm$ standard deviation across five independent cross-validation splits, each using a different random seed to assign presets to train, validation, and test sets in an 80/10/10 ratio stratified by timbre and pitch register. In V3, the split is done at preset level: all three pitch renderings of the same preset go into the same partition, so the model is never evaluated on a pitch variant of a preset it trained on. Reporting across five splits shows how much the result depends on which presets end up in the test set, rather than presenting a single run that may have been lucky or unlucky.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent Per-parameter MAE is reported separately for each of the 16 parameters on the best-performing split. This breaks the global average down into individual errors, making it possible to see which parameters the model predicts consistently well and which it struggles with. Oscillator wave frame parameters are expected to be harder to predict than envelope times, and the per-parameter breakdown makes this gap explicit.

\par \vspace{0.3cm}
\noindent The second metric is MSS, which checks whether the predicted parameters actually reproduce the original sound when fed back into Vital. For each test sample, the predicted vector $\hat{\mathbf{x}}$ is rendered through Vital at the same MIDI pitch, producing a re-rendered clip $\hat{y}$. The MSS loss between the original audio $y$ and the re-render $\hat{y}$ is the sum of L1 distances between their log-mel spectrograms across multiple time-frequency scales:

\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{MSS}}(y,\, \hat{y}) = \sum_{s} \left\| \log S_s(y) - \log S_s(\hat{y}) \right\|_1
\label{eq:mss}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $S_s(\cdot)$ is the mel spectrogram at scale $s$. A lower MSS means the re-rendered audio is spectrally closer to the original. Because Vital is non-differentiable, MSS is not used as a training objective — it is a post-hoc check that the predicted parameters sound right, not just that they are numerically close in the normalised parameter space. It is computed on a shared 100-sample test set to keep render times manageable.
